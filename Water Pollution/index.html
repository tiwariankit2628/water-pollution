Water Pollution

Water pollution occurs when harmful substances—often chemicals or microorganisms—contaminate a stream, river, lake, ocean, aquifer, or other body of water, degrading water quality and rendering it toxic to humans or the environment.

Causes

Water is uniquely vulnerable to pollution. Known as a “universal solvent,” water is able to dissolve more substances than any other liquid on earth. Water pollution can usually be attributed to one of four sources: sewage, industry, agriculture, and urban runoff including stormwater. For example, releasing inadequately treated wastewater into natural waters can lead to degradation of these aquatic ecosystems. Water pollution can also lead to water-borne diseases for people using polluted water for drinking, bathing, washing or irrigation.

Preventions

Reduce your plastic consumption and reuse or recycle plastic when you can. Properly dispose of chemical cleaners, oils, and nonbiodegradable items to keep them from going down the drain. Maintain your car so it does not leak oil, antifreeze, or coolant. Be mindful of anything you pour into storm sewers, since that waste often won’t be treated before being released into local waterways. If you notice a storm sewer blocked by litter, clean it up to keep that trash out of the water. Don't put fertilizer on the grass right before it rains. The chemicals will wash into storm drains and waterways.

Facts

Approximately 785 million people in the world do not have access to clean drinking water because of pollution. Around 70% of the industrial waste is dumped into the water bodies where they pollute the usable water supply. Industry dumps an estimated 300-400 MT of polluted waste into water bodies every year. It includes millions of tonnes of heavy metals, solvents, toxic sludge and other wastes. Fourteen billion pounds of garbage, mostly plastic, is dumped into the ocean every year. 15 million children under the age of five die each year because of diseases caused by drinking water

Types of Water Pollution

Water pollution occurs when harmful substances contaminate water bodies, affecting the ecosystem, human health, and the environment. There are several types of water pollution, each defined by the source and nature of contaminants.

Chemical Pollution

Chemical pollutants include heavy metals (such as lead, mercury, and arsenic), agricultural chemicals (fertilizers and pesticides), and industrial discharge. These substances can accumulate in aquatic organisms and enter the food chain, causing toxic effects in humans and wildlife.
Researches

* Research by Alloway (1995) highlights the dangers posed by heavy metals such as lead, mercury, cadmium, and arsenic, which can accumulate in aquatic organisms and bioaccumulate in the food chain.

Biological Pollution

Biological pollution is caused by pathogens from sewage and agricultural runoff, leading to diseases such as cholera, dysentery, and hepatitis. The presence of bacteria, viruses, and protozoa in water can severely impact public health.According to the World Health Organization (WHO, 2018), approximately 2 billion people lack access to safe drinking water, leading to outbreaks of diseases like cholera and typhoid.Studies have demonstrated the presence of fecal indicator bacteria, such as E. coli, in polluted water, signaling contamination and potential health risks.

Thermal Pollution

Thermal pollution occurs when water bodies are heated by industrial processes, power plants, or other sources, leading to changes in temperature and ecosystem disruption. This can affect aquatic life and disrupt the natural balance of water bodies.Thermal pollution results from industrial processes that discharge heated water into natural water bodies, which can decrease oxygen levels and alter aquatic life cycles.A study by Matz et al. (2010) highlights how thermal pollution disrupts the reproductive cycles of fish and decreases dissolved oxygen levels in water.

Effects of Water Pollution

Water Pollution has extensive impacts on ecosystems,human health,economics,aqatic life,Habitat degradation,Social and cultural impact(Resource conflicts and cultural degradation) and effects on drinking water quality.

Human Health Risks

Waterbone Diseases:Contaminants, including bacteria (like E. coli), viruses, and parasites, can cause diseases like cholera, dysentery, and hepatitis. These illnesses are especially prevalent in areas with poor sanitation, where people may use contaminated water for drinking and cooking.

Chemical Contamination: Chemicals like lead, arsenic, and mercury can accumulate in human bodies, causing long-term health issues, including neurological damage, developmental delays in children, and cancers.

Ecosystem Damage

Aquatic Life:Pollutants, such as heavy metals, pesticides, and industrial chemicals, are toxic to aquatic organisms. These contaminants can impair growth, cause reproductive issues, and even lead to the extinction of sensitive species.

Biodiversity Loss:Polluted water can disrupt food chains. When primary producers, like algae and plants, are affected by pollutants, it impacts organisms at all levels of the food web, reducing biodiversity and resilience within ecosystems.

Economic Impact

Healthcare Costs:Treating waterborne illnesses and health problems due to contaminated water puts financial strain on individuals and healthcare systems, especially in developing countries.

Agricultural Impact: Polluted water used for irrigation can reduce crop yields or introduce contaminants into the food supply. Heavy metals and toxic pollutants from contaminated water can accumulate in crops, harming human consumers and resulting in financial losses for farmers.
